Just read something worth your time: "The Architect Reborn." — by Paul Preiss.
Three things stayed with me.

First, the altitude. This article takes a well-documented look at how our profession has evolved — and at the cognitive biases that crept in along the way. As architects, we rarely get to see our own field from that height. This kind of mirror is uncomfortable. It is also necessary. Most of us watched those biases take hold without pushing back — witnesses more than actors. That is worth sitting with.

Second, the forging. Paul is not solo-leveling here — no manga protagonist ascending alone. What he has built with BTABoK is a community endeavor: we rise together. Now folding in AI — its promises and its traps — with clear eyes and rigorous intent. It reminds me of the great katana smiths: a lifetime of work to shape one blade that matters. That kind of dedication does not hurry.

Third, a word of caution — mine. We have a proven talent for taking something meaningful and turning it into a trend — then riding that trend until the meaning is gone. Agile did not fail because the ideas were wrong. It was crowded out by the noise of people attaching the label to whatever they were already doing. I would hate to see "architect" follow the same path. I already see it in my own company, where "Architect" and "Sr. Architect" are seniority grades with no real connection to the craft. That is not elevation. That is inflation — and it plants a new perception bias right at the root.

So yes, we need architects. I evangelize for the profession in my own small way. But a craft's value is not measured by how many people hold the title. It is measured by the standard those people uphold. The architecture profession is no exception — if anything, the bar here matters more than elsewhere.

This is not about defending a career track. It is about defending a standard.

Thank you Paul Preiss for putting this into words. And thank you to every architect who carries this quietly every day — raising the standard without waiting for permission, building the craft from the inside. This kind of work — patient, rigorous, community-first — is exactly what the profession needs more of. It matters.
